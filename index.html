Anatomy of a Scam

Look Familiar?

Most modern scams follow a highly engineered psychological script. If you've seen messages like these, you are being targeted.

1. The "Wrong Number"

An innocent-looking mistake to start the conversation.

"Hey, are we still on for golf at 10 AM tomorrow? Can't wait to catch up!"
"Hi Lisa, my niece sent me your contact info. Are you free to help with catering?"
"Hi! Is this Mary? I'm Sarah's friend from the bookstore."

2. Trust Building

Shifting from an error to a deep "fated" friendship.

"Well, since we texted by mistake, maybe it's fate! Nice to meet you, I'm David."
"I’m sorry for bothering you, but you seem really nice. Can we stay in touch?"
"I was just out walking my dog by the beach, here's a photo! What are you up to?"

3. The Pivot

The "Meat" of the scam. Guaranteed returns on crypto or DeFi.

"My uncle in Hong Kong showed me this new algorithm for crypto that is way more stable."
"I just made $15,000 this week working 30 mins a day on my phone!"
"I use a secure private node to trade. The returns are absolutely guaranteed."

This is the start to a "pig butchering" scam, one of the most sophisticated long-term investment scams. Here is what the full scam looks like and what you need to be aware of:

1
The "Wrong Number" Hook: Scammers initiate contact via unsolicited text messages disguised as an accidental text, or through dating and professional apps.
2
The Grooming Phase: They build intense platonic or romantic relationships over weeks or months without immediately asking for money.
3
The Soft Pivot: The scammer casually mentions their own immense financial success trading cryptocurrency to spark the victim's curiosity.
4
The Controlled Platform: The victim is directed to download a fake app or use a website that perfectly mirrors a legitimate trading platform.
5
Fabricated Gains: The platform displays fake, skyrocketing profits. Scammers may even allow a small initial cash withdrawal to build absolute confidence.
6
The Trap: When the victim tries to withdraw their full balance, the platform demands massive fake "IRS taxes" or "insurance fees" to unlock the funds. Once the victim runs out of money, the scammer disappears.

Knowledge Base

Empower yourself with deep insights into how scammers operate.

Am I Being Scammed?

  • Urgent demands for action:
    Example: "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours if you do not click this link." Legitimate entities will never rush you into a panic.
  • Requests for unusual payment methods:
    Example: Being asked to pay the IRS with Apple Gift Cards, or being guided to a Bitcoin ATM to send money for "safe keeping."
  • Unsolicited tech support calls:
    Example: A pop-up on your screen claiming you have a virus and must call "Microsoft Support" at a provided 1-800 number immediately.

Immediate Next Steps

  • Cease all communication:
    Do not explain yourself or argue. Simply hang up the phone or block the number/email immediately.
  • Freeze involved bank accounts:
    Call your bank's official fraud department immediately. You can legally freeze your accounts to stop outgoing wires.
    Official 24/7 Fraud Hotlines (USA):
    • Chase: 1-800-935-9935
    • Bank of America: 1-800-432-1000
    • Wells Fargo: 1-800-869-3557
    • Citibank: 1-888-248-4226
    (Always verify numbers on the back of your official debit/credit card before calling.)
  • Change affected passwords:
    Log out of all devices and change your email and banking passwords from a safe, separate device (like your smartphone, if your computer was compromised).

Future Prevention

  • Enable 2-Factor Authentication (2FA):
    Use an authenticator app (like Google Authenticator or Authy) rather than SMS text messages, to prevent SIM-swap attacks.
  • Verify identities through a secondary channel:
    If your "boss" texts you demanding a favor, message them on your company's Slack or call their known number to confirm it's actually them.

The "Stop, Secure, Share" Protocol

  • Stop: Immediately cease all communication with the scammer. Do not try to "get them back" or argue; just cut the line.
  • Secure: Lock down your assets. Change passwords, freeze your credit, and notify your bank or the platform where the scam occurred.
  • Share: Break the silence. Reporting the scam to authorities (like the FTC or IC3) and telling your story helps remove the stigma and warns others.

Common Scams & Current Threats

Click on any of the scam categories below to learn how they work, the major red flags to watch out for, and recent real-world examples.

Pig Butchering (Sha Zhu Pan) & Romance Scams

Originating in China around 2016, "Pig Butchering" is a devastating hybrid of romance/friendship scams and fake crypto investments. Scammers "fatten" victims with false affection before "slaughtering" their finances.

The Anatomy of the Scam:
  • The Lure: "Accidental" wrong number text ("Hi Sarah, it was lovely to see you.").
  • Fattening: Weeks of building a deep emotional or romantic bond, sharing fake lavish photos.
  • The Pivot: Claiming huge profits from an "uncle's insider tip" on a secret crypto platform.
  • The Trap: Victims deposit funds into a fake exchange and even withdraw a small "profit" to build trust.
  • The Slaughter: Demanding massive "tax fees" to withdraw full funds, then disappearing.
Red Flag Example: A text from an unknown highly attractive person wanting to be friends, who mentions trading cryptocurrency within a few weeks.
Crypto & Day Trading "Gurus"

Fraudsters sell fake courses, "signal groups," or trading bots with guaranteed massive returns, exploiting vulnerable demographics expecting quick wealth.

Major Red Flags:
  • Lifestyle Marketing: Showing off rented Lamborghinis or AirBnB mansions to sell a $999 Discord course.
  • "Guaranteed" Risk-Free Returns: No legitimate trading strategy is risk-free. Anyone promising it is lying.
Fact Check: Peer-reviewed studies show that 97% of amateur day traders lose money over a 300-day period. (FGV Study).
Data Breaches & Text Phishing (Smishing)

Criminal syndicates like ShinyHunters steal millions of user records from cloud databases (e.g. Salesforce) and sell them. Other scammers use this data to send highly targeted fake text messages (Smishing) or emails claiming to be your bank or the post office.

How to protect yourself:
  • Never click links in unexpected "Package Delivery" or "Bank Alert" texts.
  • Freeze your credit with all major bureaus.
  • Use a Password Manager and never reuse passwords.
AI Deepfakes & Voice Cloning ("Hi Grandma")

Scammers now use just 3 seconds of audio from public social media to perfectly clone a loved one's voice. They call relatives mimicking distress (e.g., "Hi Grandma, I'm in jail, wire me bail money via Zelle").

How to protect yourself:
  • Set up a verbal "safe word" with your family.
  • If a family member calls from an unknown number demanding money, hang up and dial their real saved number to verify it.
Secondary Recovery Scams

A cruel "scam within a scam." After you lose money to fraud, a different scammer contacts you pretending to be the FBI, a lawyer, or a "white-hat hacker" guaranteeing they can recover your stolen crypto... for an upfront fee.

Remember: Lost cryptocurrency is almost never recoverable by private individuals. Government agencies will never ask for an upfront fee to investigate fraud.

The Psychology of Fraud

Scammers don't just hack computers; they hack human psychology. Financial fraud is fundamentally an exercise in social engineering, relying on cognitive biases to bypass your logical thinking centers.

1. The Urgency Trap (Under the Ether)

AARP fraud researchers call it the "ether" — a state of high emotional arousal (fear or extreme excitement). Creating a panic shuts down the logical prefrontal cortex, forcing a fight-or-flight decision.

2. Authority Bias

Imposter scams (the 2nd highest fraud category reported to the FTC) pretend to be the IRS, Police, or a CEO. They exploit our ingrained sociological tendency to comply with perceived authority.

3. The "Free Lunch" & Reciprocity

Exploiting human greed or desperation through promises of guaranteed returns. Scammers often start by doing a small "favor" to trigger the human obligation of reciprocity.

4. Social Media & FOMO

Fake, lavish lifestyles posted online manufacture a continuous "Fear Of Missing Out" (FOMO). By making people feel like they are falling behind or not successful enough, they strip away critical thinking and push victims into taking massive, uncharacteristic financial risks to catch up.

Fact Check Sources: AARP Fraud Watch Network Research | FTC Imposter Scam Warnings

The Modern "Scamdemic"

Fraud has skyrocketed over the past few years due to increased remote connectivity during COVID-19 and the unchecked rise of Generative AI tools.

$12.5B Total reported losses to the FBI IC3 in 2023 (a 22% increase).
$10B+ Reported directly to the FTC in 2023 alone.
$4.6B Lost specifically to investment scams (the highest category).

The COVID-19 Catalyst

The pandemic was a perfect storm for cybercriminals. The sudden shift in global behavior created systemic vulnerabilities that scammers are still exploiting today:

1. The Isolation Factor

With lockdowns separating families, extreme loneliness set in—particularly among older demographics. Scammers rushed to fill this void via dating apps. Because people couldn't legally meet in-person anyway, the classic "I can't meet you" excuse became entirely plausible, leading to record-breaking spikes in Romance Scams.

2. Forced Digital Migration

Overnight, millions of vulnerable individuals who had never used online banking, grocery delivery apps, or Zoom were forced onto the internet. They lacked fundamental cybersecurity education, making them completely unequipped to spot fake tech-support pop-ups or spoofed banking alerts.

3. Government Relief Phishing

The rapid rollout of stimulus checks and expanded unemployment created massive public confusion. Scammers spun up perfectly replicated federal portals and sent millions of texts claiming victims needed to "verify their identity to receive their stimulus check," harvesting unprecedented amounts of Social Security Numbers.

The AI Weaponizer

Following the lockdown era, Generative AI advanced rapidly to replace the manual labor of scamming. The FTC reports highly organized syndicates now use Deepfakes and AI Voice Cloning to terrorize families over the phone, completely bypassing the grammatical errors that used to give away phishing campaigns.
